While spreadsheet programs and text editors can be used to manually clean data, the best approach to clean data is to code it. This requires three steps:

  • Define — express in words how you intend to resolve the problem.
  • Code — turn your definitions into executable code.
  • Test —test your data to confirm that your code was properly implemented.

Dataset: Oral Insulin Phase II Clinical Trial Data

Understanding the Dataset

Diabetes’ increasing prevalence in the twenty-first century is a worry.
Patients experience symptoms such as:

  • excessive thirst
  • frequent urination
  • extreme exhaustion

Diabetes can also cause more catastrophic problems such as stroke, blindness, limb loss, renal failure, and heart attack. I will examine the phase two clinical trial data from 350 patients for Auralin, a novel unique oral insulin that is a patented capsule that potentially fix the stomach lining problem.

The purpose of phase two trials is to:

  • Test the efficacy and the dose response of a drug
  • Identify adverse reactions

In this trial, half of the patients are given Auralin, while the other 175 are given Novodra, a popular injectable insulin. I can establish whether Auralin is successful by analyzing important parameters between these two medications.

What is the purpose of Data Cleaning?

Healthcare data is infamous for including errors and being disorganized, and clinical trial data is no exception. Human errors, for example, during the patient registration procedure mean that we can have:

  • duplicate data
  • missing data
  • inaccurate data

I’m going to start by examining the quality and tidiness of this data collection, and then cleaning up all of these issues with Python and Pandas. The purpose is to produce a reliable analysis.

Preparing To Clean

Before any cleaning can begin, a copy of each piece of data must be created. All cleaning procedures will be performed on this copy, allowing me to inspect the original dirty and/or untidy dataset later.

Address Missing Data First

When checking data quality, it is usually best to deal with completeness issues first. For missing data this means:

  • Concatenate
  • Join
  • Impute, if possible

After resolving missing data, the next natural step is to clean for tidiness. Hadley Wickham, a statistician, the pioneer of tidy data, in his publication, ‘Tidy data’ (The Journal of Statistical Software, vol. 59, 2014), states the following key points:

  • Clean datasets are simple to work with.
  • Tidy datasets with data quality issues are almost always easier to clean than disorderly datasets with the same difficulties.

This means that it’s often ideal to clean structural concerns first, such as tidiness, and then clean content issues, such as quality.

There are full state names sometimes, abbreviations other times, I’ll apply a function that converts full state name to state abbreviation for California, New York, Illinois, Florida, and Nebraska

Strip the letter ‘u’ in start dose and end dose and convert those columns to data type integer.

Is Auralin Effective?

Using Key Metrics to Compare

After analyzing and cleansing the clinical trial data, I’m ready to see how Auralin, a proposed new oral insulin, compares to Novodra, an injectable insulin.

Adverse Effects

For Auralin to pass this Phase II clinical trial it must be deemed safe, and the adverse reactions to it is encouraging.

These adverse reactions were previously separate tables, but I joined them to the treatments table to make this analysis possible. The numbers of each adverse event are very similar between the two drugs, Auralin and Novodra. One exception is Auralin, the oral insulin, which causes throat irritation since it is administered orally and goes through the mouth before reaching the stomach. Another side effect of Novodra, which is an injectable insulin, is injection site discomfort, which is a well-known side effect of injectable insulin due to needles. This is one of the primary reasons for the development of oral insulin.

In these horizontal bar charts, the counts are more visible.

The dose change is critical because if the new oral insulin requires a higher dosage to be effective, the manufacturer may decide not to bring it to market since it is not commercially viable.

The dosage information was hidden in two columns in the treatments table, auralin and novodra, with start and finish doses in each column and treatment values in the column headers. By melting the treatment variable down to its own column, I was able to convert this to a more readable format and separate the start dose and end dose. Again, Auralin’s results are promising. In comparison to Novodra patients, who required 0.4 units less insulin, individuals treated with Auralin required 8 more units of insulin on average to achieve a safe, constant blood sugar level. Because I understood that oral insulin has a harder time getting into the bloodstream through the stomach lining, and eight units more isn’t a major thing, Auralin requiring 8 more units was expected.
